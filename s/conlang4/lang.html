Phonology and orthography

Phonotactics

The syllable structure is C(C)V(N). The two consecutive Cs can take any of the following patterns:

  • {p t̪ c q ʔ} followed by {ɬ̪ ɕ h}, except for /qɕ/
  • {ɕ ʑ} followed by {m n̪}

The syllable /qhi/ is illegal.

Assimilation

/h/ is [h] before /a/, [ɸʷ] before /u/, and [ç] before /i/.

Trailing nasals assimilate to the following place of articulation, but not across word boundaries. (However, ⟨g⟩ and ⟨ng⟩ are pronounced identically rather than with a lengthened nasal.)

The vowels /u i a/ behave as follows:

Around uvulars: [o e ä] (including /qɬ̪/, /qh/)
Around palatals: [u i ɜ] (including /cɬ̪/, /ch/, /Xɕ/ but not /ɕN/, /ʑN/)
Elsewhere: [u i ä]

In a sequence of uvular-vowel-palatal or palatal-vowel-uvular, the vowel behaves in the uvular context.

In diphthongs, the first vowel assimilates normally, and the second vowel changes depending on the first:

  • /au/ → [äo] or [ɜu]
  • /ua/ → [oä] or [uɜ]
  • /ai/ → [äe] or [ɜi]
  • /ia/ → [eä] or [iɜ]

Prosody

Stress is always on the first syllable. Multisyllabic words always contain one long phone—either the first vowel or the second consonant. In the orthography, the vowel is doubled to indicate the former; otherwise, the latter is assumed. In diphthongs, it is the second vowel that is lengthened (and doubled in the orthography).

The first syllable of a word following a preposition or other grammatical particle is unstressed.

Morphology

Elements of the morphology and syntax were inspired by Russian, Hindustani, and Navajo.

Nouns

Nouns are assigned to one of four genders based on animacy:

  • animate: humans and other animals (typically end in -a)
  • inanimate dynamic: plants, natural forces, machines (typically end in -n)
  • inanimate static: other physically tangible objects (typically end in -u)
  • abstract: ideas, concepts, etc. (typically end in -i)

The nonstandard glossing abbreviations an, dy, st, and ab will be used for the remainder of this document.

Nouns are also declined for case:

  • nominative: the subject of an intransitive or transitive perfective verb
  • absolutive: the subject of an intransitive imperfective verb or the object of a transitive imperfective verb
  • direct: the object of a transitive perfective verb or the subject of an intransitive imperfective verb
  • prepositional: used with locative-type prepositions
  • instrumental: indicates means of accomplishing the action

The "direct" case is in reality a conflation of the accusative and ergative cases. Here is a more visual representation of the split-ergativity system:

Perfective Imperfective
S A O S A O
nom nom dir abs dir abs

In this way, the noun cases present in a sentence serve to unambiguously identify whether the verb has perfective or imperfective aspect. Thus, no other grammatical distinction of aspect is made.

Verbs

Verbs are required to be marked for evidentiality/mood:

  • experience: direct observation on the part of the speaker, witness
  • hearsay: reported information, relayed, secondhand
  • inferential: presumably, probably, must be, expectation, conclusion
  • speculative: maybe, possibly, postulation, assumption
  • imperative: also used for performatives
  • subjunctive: also used for conditionals
  • gnomic: also used for interrogatives

Verbs are also conjugated to agree with gender of the subject and object. No grammaticalized tense markers exist, and aspect is distinguished as described in the Nouns section.

Several applicative markers exist that promote an inferior case to the nominative or absolutive case:

dir prep ins
-fi -hua -pham

The direct applicative is essentially a passive/antipassive marker. With the prepositional applicative, the preposition in question comes immediately before the verb. The nominative/absolutive case can be recovered with the particle tan followed by a noun in the replaced case.

Verbs can be made into nouns with the -ni suffix, which can be analyzed as a complementizer (comp). TODO example. If the verb has arguments that cannot be easily inferred, they simply follow the verb (although one would normally not do this when the desired interpretation is obvious; for example, I like to walk would be translated as TODO rather than TODO).

The relativizer (rel) is the suffix -vi. This causes a verb to modify the previous noun; it specifies that the noun fits the role of the nominative or absolutive case (as appropriate) for the given verb. To specify that the noun takes any other case, one of the applicatives described above can be used.

Both the complementizer and the relativizer can stand alone as their own words, in which case they indicate the termination of the subclause. However, these words are typically omitted unless doing so would cause the sentence to become ambiguous (and even then, they are usually only used for particularly confusing sentences).

Adjectives

There are none. Adjectives are simply relativized verbs. For example, the black cat is expressed as the cat that is black. The same is true for possessives.

Compounds

Words can be compounded by juxtaposition, which triggers some sound changes and mutations (reflected in the orthography). This juxtaposition causes the latter word to modify the former in an intrinsic way (e.g. modifying the noun for fish with the verb for to be red does not mean red fish but rather something like salmon).

The sound changes, which are mostly forms of lenition, are as follows:

  • The long vowel or consonant is shortened to its normal length.
  • The initial consonant of the latter word undergoes the following shift:
    • /p/ → {/pɕ/ before /i/, /pɬ̪/ otherwise} → {/ɕ/, /ɬ̪/}
    • /t̪/ → {/t̪ɬ̪/ before /a/, /t̪ɕ/ otherwise} → {/ɬ̪/, /ɕ/}
    • /ɬ̪/ → /ɮ̪/ → /n̪/
    • /c/ → /ɕ/ → /ʑ/ → /ʑm/
    • {/ɕm/, /ɕn̪/} → {/ʑm/, /ʑn̪/} → {/m/, /n̪/} → /∅/
    • /q/ → /q͡χ/ → /ɴ͡ɢ/ → /ʔ/
    • {/ʔ/, /Xh/} → /h/ → /∅/
  • If the preceding step results in an illegal diphthong, an epenthetic /ʔ/ is inserted.
  • If the last syllable of the former word is uvular and the first syllable of the latter word is palatal or vice versa, a /m/ or /n̪/ (if the former word ends in a palatal or uvular respectively) followed by the first vowel of the latter word is inserted between them.
  • The last vowel of the former word changes to whatever vowel now follows it.

All steps involving vowel harmony (the last two steps) will only copy the first vowel in a diphthong.

More than two words may be juxtaposed, in which case "A B C" indicates that "C" modifies "A B" rather than "B C" modifying "A". If the latter meaning is desired, the first step of the sound changes above is skipped (that is, the long vowel or consonant remains long). If this is the case and the word has a long consonant, it must be doubled in the orthography to indicate that it remains long in this position.

Syntax

Word order

Word order is generally strongly head-initial. Verbs come before the subject and object, but the subject and object are required to be ordered in terms of the animacy hierarchy (that is, if the subject is more animate than the object, it comes first, and vice versa). If the subject and object are of the same animacy, the one in the non-direct case comes first.

Modifiers also come after the phrase they are modifying. This is true for relative clauses and compounds (see the Morphology section). Prepositions precede the noun that they pertain to.
